Solar System — An Illustrated Guide

A concise tour through our Sun's family: the star, planets, dwarf worlds, and countless small bodies that orbit together through space.

Overview

The Solar System consists of the Sun — a G-type main-sequence star — and everything bound to it by gravity. This includes eight major planets, five officially recognized dwarf planets, moons of many sizes, and a huge number of smaller objects such as asteroids and comets. The system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust.

Understanding the Solar System helps us place Earth in context and informs planetary science, astronomy, and the search for life beyond our planet.

The Sun

The Sun accounts for more than 99.8% of the mass in the Solar System. Its intense gravity holds the system together, and its energy powers planetary climates and life on Earth. The Sun is a dynamic, magnetically active star with a visible surface (the photosphere), an ever-changing atmosphere, and phenomena such as sunspots and solar flares.

Inner (Terrestrial) Planets

The four inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — are rocky worlds with solid surfaces. They are smaller and denser than the outer planets and have varied atmospheres and geologic histories. Earth is the only known world with life and liquid surface water, while Mars and Venus show fascinating, contrasting climates and histories.

Exploration of inner planets has included orbiters, landers, and rovers that study atmosphere, geology, and potential habitability.

Outer (Giant) Planets

Beyond the asteroid belt lie the giant planets: Jupiter and Saturn (gas giants) and Uranus and Neptune (ice giants). These massive worlds have deep atmospheres, powerful storms, ring systems, and many moons, some of which — like Jupiter's Europa and Saturn's Enceladus — may host subsurface oceans.

Dwarf Planets & Small Bodies

Dwarf planets such as Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres share space with thousands of smaller objects. The asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter contains many rocky bodies, while icy comets originate in the distant Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud. These small bodies are remnants of planet formation and carry clues about the early Solar System.

Exploration & Future

Humanity has explored the Solar System with telescopes and spacecraft for decades. Landmark missions like Voyager, Cassini, New Horizons, and Mars rovers transformed our understanding. Future priorities include returning samples to Earth, exploring ocean-bearing moons, and advancing human exploration to the Moon and Mars.

Continued exploration will refine our knowledge of planetary formation, habitability, and the potential for life elsewhere in the Solar System.

Quick Facts

  • Age: ~4.6 billion years
  • Planets: 8 (Mercury → Neptune)
  • Dwarf planets: 5+ officially recognized
  • Largest planet: Jupiter
  • Closest star: The Sun
